The model

What PolicyEngine does

Open-source tax-benefit microsimulation for the US, UK, and Canada.

10,000+ parameters

Federal and state tax codes, benefit programs, credits, and deductions encoded as structured data.

Household simulation

Calculate any household's taxes, benefits, and net income under current law or policy reforms.

Population analysis

Run reforms against representative survey data to estimate budgetary and distributional impacts.

AGPL-3.0 Python REST API US · UK · Canada
PolicyEngine is three things. First, a structured database of over 10,000 policy parameters — every federal and state tax rate, benefit threshold, income limit, and deduction. Second, a simulation engine that calculates any household's taxes and benefits. Third, population-level microsimulation using enhanced CPS microdata. Everything is open source under AGPL-3.0.

Parameters, variables, tests, citations, edge cases — each from dozens of pages of statutes and regulations. To study how AI reshapes the safety net, we first need the safety net encoded.

Here's the bottleneck. Each state benefit program requires about 1,500 lines of code. Parameters need legal citations. Variables need test cases. It takes 2-3 weeks per program manually. Multiply that by 50+ programs across 50 states and you see the scale problem. To model how AI impacts inequality through the safety net, we first need the safety net fully encoded.

The challenge

Three mechanisms of uncertainty

AI could reshape market incomes through three channels:

📈

Wage inequality

Early-career workers in AI-exposed jobs saw a 16% employment decline; senior workers gained 6-12%. Direction depends on which tasks are complemented vs. automated.

Stanford DEL 2025
🏢

Capital-labor shift

Labor share of income could decline 0.5-1.6 pp per doubling of AI innovation. Capital income and wealth inequality always increase with AI adoption.

IMF 2025
👥

Labor displacement

11.7% of the US labor market is automatable today (~$1.2T in wage value). Labor force participation projected to drop to 78% by 2030.

MIT Iceberg · Metaculus
Three mechanisms. Wage inequality — some workers complemented, others replaced. Capital-labor shift — AI increases returns to capital, always increases wealth inequality. Labor displacement — 11.7% automatable today, 1.2 trillion in wage value. Each has enormous uncertainty about magnitude and timing.

The IMF finds AI might reduce wage inequality but always increases wealth inequality. And Brookings warns AI could erode the entire basis of labor-based taxation.
The gap

Why current analysis falls short

Single baseline forecasts

CBO and traditional scoring institutions operate under one economic scenario. AI-driven change involves profound uncertainty about magnitude, timing, and distribution.

Policy depends on trajectory

A UBI might be transformative under rapid displacement but unnecessary under gradual augmentation. Robustness depends on which AI trajectory materializes.

Traditional policy analysis reports only point estimates. AI-driven economic change requires modeling ranges of scenarios to show how policy impacts vary across different trajectories.

Multiple AI scenarios Probabilistic forecasts Robust policy analysis
CBO operates under a single baseline. That worked when the economy was predictable. But AI introduces profound uncertainty. A UBI might be transformative if displacement is rapid, but wasteful if AI mostly augments. We need probabilistic analysis across multiple trajectories.
