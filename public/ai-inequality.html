The third ingredient

Labor supply elasticities

How people adjust their work in response to policy changes.

Intensive margin

Hours worked. When marginal tax rates change, workers adjust how much they work. PolicyEngine uses CBO’s substitution elasticity of 0.25 for labor income.

Extensive margin

Participation. Changes in average tax rates affect whether people work at all. CBO estimates an income elasticity of -0.05, capturing decisions to enter or exit the labor force.

Why this matters for AI. If AI compresses wages for some occupations, the behavioral response depends on these elasticities. Workers facing lower wages may exit the labor force entirely (extensive margin) or reduce hours (intensive margin). The policy response — UBI, wage subsidies, retraining credits — interacts with these elasticities to determine the net effect.

CBO framework Substitution + income effects Configurable parameters
The third ingredient — dynamics. How people respond to policy changes. Two margins: intensive, how much you work, and extensive, whether you work at all. We use CBO's elasticity framework. Why does this matter for AI? If AI compresses wages, these elasticities determine whether people reduce hours or leave the workforce entirely. And the policy response — UBI, wage subsidies, retraining credits — interacts with these elasticities. You can't evaluate policy without modeling the behavioral response.

Putting the ingredients together

The challenge

Three mechanisms of uncertainty

AI could reshape market incomes through three channels:

Wage inequality

Early-career workers in AI-exposed jobs saw a 16% employment decline; senior workers gained 6-12%. Direction depends on which tasks are complemented vs. automated.

Stanford DEL 2025

Capital-labor shift

Labor share of income could decline 0.5-1.6 pp per doubling of AI innovation. Capital income and wealth inequality always increase with AI adoption.

IMF 2025

Labor displacement

11.7% of the US labor market is automatable today (~$1.2T in wage value). Labor force participation projected to drop to 78% by 2030.

MIT Iceberg · Metaculus
Three mechanisms. Wage inequality — some workers complemented, others replaced. Capital-labor shift — AI increases returns to capital, always increases wealth inequality. Labor displacement — 11.7% automatable today, 1.2 trillion in wage value. Each has enormous uncertainty about magnitude and timing.

Initial results

What if AI doubles capital income?

Scaling positive capital income 1x–5x in PolicyEngine’s microsimulation:

Multiplier Capital share Net Gini SPM poverty Top 10% share Extra tax revenue
Baseline 11% 0.507 20.5% 38.2% —
2x 20% 0.537 20.1% 41.6% +$715B
5x 38% 0.608 19.4% 50.7% +$2.9T

Poverty barely moves because capital gains concentrate at the top. But inequality surges — and the extra tax revenue creates policy space. Redistributing the 2x revenue as ~$1,776/person UBI cuts poverty from 20.5% to 16.2%.

Here are our initial results. If AI doubles capital income — which is plausible given AI companies' market cap growth — the Gini rises from 0.507 to 0.537. Poverty barely moves because capital income is concentrated at the top. But there's $715 billion in extra tax revenue. Redistribute that as a flat UBI and you cut poverty from 20.5% to 16.2%. At 5x capital, the top 10% captures over half of income.
Initial results

What if AI shifts labor to capital?

Modeling AI automation as a constant-GDP shift from labor income to capital income:

Labor → capital shift Net Gini SPM poverty Top 10% share
Baseline 0.507 20.5% 38.2%
10% shift 0.527 21.9% 40.3%
50% shift 0.623 36.0% 51.4%
50% shift + UBI 0.608 29.6% 50.4%

Unlike the capital growth scenario, labor-to-capital shift devastates poverty — 36% at a 50% shift. UBI from extra revenue only partially offsets (29.6%). The tax system itself becomes less effective: employment MTR rises from 17% to 26% as the base narrows.

The scarier scenario: labor income shifts to capital at constant GDP. A 50% shift nearly doubles poverty to 36%. UBI from extra revenue only gets it down to 29.6% — you can't fully compensate because the tax base is eroding. Employment marginal tax rates rise from 17% to 26% as fewer workers bear more of the burden. This is the scenario where current policy tools are most inadequate.
